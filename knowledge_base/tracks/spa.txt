Circuit de Spa-Francorchamps - Circuit Guide

CIRCUIT OVERVIEW

Spa-Francorchamps is located in the Ardennes forest of Belgium and is the longest circuit on the Formula 1 calendar at 7.004 km. The circuit features 20 corners and significant elevation changes, combining fast flowing sections with technical low-speed chicanes. It is renowned for its iconic Eau Rouge and Raidillon complex, the long Kemmel Straight, and unpredictable weather that can change from dry to wet within a single lap. The power-hungry first and third sectors are almost flat-out, while the middle sector features medium to high-speed corners that demand aerodynamic compromise.

CAR SETUP

Spa demands a balance between low drag for the long straights and sufficient downforce for the fast corners such as Blanchimont and Pouhon. A very low aerodynamic setup helps maintain top speed through the fast sectors. Tyre warm-up is critical at Spa, especially in cold or wet conditions. The high energy nature of the circuit demands careful tyre management throughout a stint.

TURN 1 — LA SOURCE

A tight hairpin at the top of the hill immediately after the start-finish straight. One of the most critical overtaking opportunities on the circuit, especially on the first lap. Brake approximately 100 metres before the corner just after passing under the footbridge. Brake hard in a straight line and downshift. Turn in smoothly, aiming for a very late apex to carry as much speed as possible down to Eau Rouge. Be careful with throttle application on exit as wheelspin ruins the run down to Eau Rouge and Raidillon.

TURN 2-4 — EAU ROUGE AND RAIDILLON

One of the most iconic corner complexes in motorsport. The car goes downhill through Eau Rouge and immediately uphill through Raidillon at very high speed. Modern F1 cars take this flat out in dry conditions. Any lift here is a significant time loss and represents a failure of commitment.

After La Source, position the car on the left of the track as it descends towards Eau Rouge. Go as close to the left-hand wall as possible and stay there. Turn left through Eau Rouge, keeping the car as straight as possible through the compression at the bottom. Then turn right up through Raidillon, not too early. The goal is to go up in a straight line and apply maximum throttle for the long Kemmel Straight ahead. Any correction or instability through Raidillon costs significant lap time.

KEMMEL STRAIGHT

One of the longest straights in Formula 1. Drivers reach speeds exceeding 300 km/h. This is the main DRS zone and one of the best overtaking opportunities on the calendar. Move to the far left of the track on approach to Les Combes to maximise the braking zone.

TURN 5-7 — LES COMBES

A right-left-right chicane at the end of the Kemmel Straight. Heavy braking is required at approximately the 100-metre board. Downshift from sixth gear to third or second gear. The key is to treat Les Combes as a complex rather than individual corners. Getting a good exit from the final right-hander is the priority, which dictates the approach to the preceding corners.

Hug the left-hand side of the track on approach. At the first right-hander, aim for a late apex. Do not stay on the right too long as the left-hander that follows is more important. Cut the inside kerb of the left-hander to help rotate the car. Carry this momentum through to the third corner which is the fastest of the sequence and exit onto the straight with maximum speed.

TURN 9 — BRUXELLES

A 180-degree right-hander. Begin braking when the kerb on the left starts. Aim for a late apex. Stay right on exit for the following double right-hander.
