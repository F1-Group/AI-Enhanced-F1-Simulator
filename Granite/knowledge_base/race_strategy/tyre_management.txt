Tyre Management in Formula 1 Racing

DEGRADATION VS WEAR

Degradation and wear are two distinct but linked concepts. Degradation is a thermal performance loss caused by the tyres overheating or underheating. Wear is the physical consumption of the tyre tread. Both must be managed simultaneously. Changes in setup can solve one problem but create an issue elsewhere. The goal is to extract maximum performance from the tyre without stressing its limitations.

TYRE OPERATING WINDOW

Modern F1 tyres have a relatively narrow optimal operating window. Tyre loads, running pressures, and temperatures must be controlled and monitored carefully. If the tyre is outside its operating window, grip falls off significantly. Getting the tyre to perform within its operating window is critical to both qualifying and race performance.

TYRE GIVE-UP

For most racing tyres, the first one or two laps are the fastest laps those tyres will ever produce. After this initial peak, performance gradually degrades over the stint. Teams monitor this degradation trend using sector-by-sector timing data, which updates every few seconds and allows engineers to identify the precise moment the tyre compound begins losing performance.

Once a rival has pitted and switched to a different compound, engineers can immediately read the pace delta from their sector times and make informed decisions about when to pit their own driver.

HEAT CYCLES

Every heat cycle changes a tyre, generally making it harder, less flexible, and less adhesive. Race tyres can lose effectiveness before the tread wears through if they go through too many heat cycles. The first heat cycle is called scrubbing. After scrubbing, the tyre's rubber chemistry has changed and it will not return to its original state.

TYRE WARM-UP

Cold tyres have significantly less grip than tyres at operating temperature. At the start of a stint, drivers must warm up their tyres gradually. Aggressive inputs on cold tyres cause graining and can permanently damage the tyre's rubber surface before it has reached optimal temperature.

GRAINING

Graining occurs when the soft, adhesive tread surface digs into the track texture, deforms into waves, and as sliding continues the waves turn over, wearing rubber off the upstream side. The result is a grainy texture on the tyre surface that reduces grip.

Graining is most likely to occur when the driver is overly aggressive on cold tyres, when the car has a poorly balanced setup with excess understeer, or when track grip levels are very low. A well-balanced car generates more even tyre loading and is less prone to graining. Once a graining pattern develops on a tyre surface, it is difficult to wear away.

BLISTERING

Blistering occurs when the tyre overheats internally. The heat is generated at the interface between the belt plies and the tread. When tyre temperature exceeds the compound's thermal limit, the rubber melts locally, forming a blister on the surface. Unlike graining, blistering permanently damages the tyre and cannot be recovered.

Soft compound tyres are more prone to blistering because their lower wear rate means more heat is generated internally rather than being dissipated through rubber removal. High inflation pressures and high track temperatures increase the risk of blistering.
